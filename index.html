5.1
Learning Goal One

What is stress, and how is it related to stressors and strains?

Stress depends on both the nature of a demand and the person who confronts it — so the same situation stresses different people differently.

StressStressorsStrainsTransactional theory
Core Vocabulary
Three words that anchor the whole chapter
Stress
A psychological response to demands that possess certain stakes for the person and that tax or exceed the person's capacity or resources.
Stressors
The demands that cause a person to experience stress. (Coral throughout this deck marks stressors and their negative effects.)
Strains
The negative consequences that occur when demands tax or exceed a person's capacity or resources — physiological, psychological, and behavioral.
“
Because people differ in how they perceive, evaluate, and cope with stressors, different people can experience very different levels of stress in the exact same situation.

Figure 5-1
The transactional theory of stress
It explains how stressors are perceived and appraised, and how people respond to those appraisals. Two appraisals sit at its heart:

1Primary appraisal

“Is this stressful?” You evaluate the significance and meaning of the stressor — and if it does stress you, what it implies for your personal goals and well-being.

2Secondary appraisal

“How can I cope?” You ask what should I do? and what can I do? to deal with the demand — the basis of every coping strategy in Section 5.3.

Benign job demands: job demands that tend not to be appraised as stressful. A well-trained cashier ringing routine sales doesn't find the task taxing — so it never triggers the stress response.

5.2
Learning Goal Two

What are the four main types of stressors?

Two flavors — hindrance and challenge — crossed with two domains — work and nonwork.

Hindrance × ChallengeWork × NonworkStressful life events
The Key Distinction
Not all stressors are created equal
Hindrance stressors

Hurdles that block you

Stressful demands people perceive as hindering progress toward accomplishments or goals. They most often trigger negative emotions — anxiety and anger.

Challenge stressors

Opportunities that stretch you

Stressful demands people perceive as opportunities for learning, growth, and achievement. Though exhausting, they trigger positive emotions — pride and enthusiasm.

Both types are genuinely stressful. The difference is the meaning the person assigns — and, as Section 5.5 shows, that meaning flips their effect on performance and commitment from harmful to helpful.

Work · Hindrance
Roles that get in your way

⚔Role conflict

Conflicting expectations others hold of you. A call-center rep told to be fast yet thorough can't satisfy both.

?Role ambiguity

Lack of information about what a role requires — and unpredictable consequences. Common for new employees and vague courses.

⧉Role overload

So many demanding roles that some can't be done well. Now more common than role conflict or ambiguity.

•••Daily hassles

Minor day-to-day demands that pile up — needless paperwork, broken equipment, useless emails.

Scale of the problem: 80-hour weeks are becoming the norm in investment banking, consulting, and law — yet those employees say they still couldn't finish the work even at twice the hours.
IS 340 · Chapter 5 — StressWork hindrance stressors · pp. 126–127
Work · Challenge
Demands worth striving for

⏱Time pressure

A strong sense that you don't quite have enough time. Stressful, yet meeting the deadline is intrinsically satisfying — like an architect watching his work rise over the skyline.

◈Work complexity

Work whose knowledge, skills, and abilities tax or exceed your current capability. Being “stretched” in a developmental assignment is worth the discomfort.

◎Work responsibility

The nature of your obligations to others. An air-traffic controller accountable for thousands of lives feels this acutely — stressful, but meaningful.

As with time pressure and complexity, people tend to evaluate high responsibility as both stressful and potentially positive — the hallmark of a challenge stressor.

Nonwork · Hindrance
Stress that spills over from outside work

⇄Work–family conflict

A special role conflict where work demands hinder family roles — or vice versa. It can spiral: home conflict makes work harder, which makes home worse.

☂Negative life events

Life changes appraised as hindrances — the death of a loved one, divorce, a jail term, or a personal illness. See the Holmes–Rahe scale ahead.

$Financial uncertainty

Uncertainty about losing livelihood, savings, or the ability to pay expenses — highly relevant in recessions and downturns.

Interestingly, both work-to-family and family-to-work conflict tend to be higher for employees who are strongly embedded in their organizations and communities.

Demands outside work that help you grow

♥Family time demands

Time committed to family activities — traveling, social events, hosting, home improvements. Demanding, yet positive.

↗Personal development

Formal education, music lessons, sports training, hobbies, local government, or volunteer work that fosters growth.

✦Positive life events

Marriage, a new family member, graduating from school — stressful in their own way, but tied to positive emotions.

The Holmes–Rahe scale (next tool) scores marriage at 50, pregnancy at 40, and an outstanding achievement at 28 — proof that even good news carries a stress load.

5.3
Learning Goal Three

How do individuals cope with stress?

After appraising a demand, people ask “What should I do?” and “What can I do?” — the secondary appraisal that drives every coping strategy.

Behavioral × CognitiveProblem × Emotion focusedSense of control
Definition
Coping = the behaviors and thoughts you use to manage demands
Coping
The behaviors and thoughts people use to manage both the stressful demands they face and the emotions associated with those demands.
Coping activities fall into four categories built from two independent dimensions:

Dimension 1 — Method

Behavioral (physical activities to deal with the situation) vs. Cognitive (the thoughts involved in dealing with it).

Dimension 2 — Focus

Problem-focused (manage the situation itself) vs. Emotion-focused (manage your emotional reactions to it).

Which focus wins?
Problem-focused vs. emotion-focused — and why it matters

✓Problem-focused coping

Behaviors and cognitions aimed at the demand itself — working harder or devising a smarter strategy. Managers want subordinates to cope this way with heavy workloads.

♻Emotion-focused coping

Managing your emotional reactions — distancing, reappraising, venting. Sometimes functional (protecting self-esteem), but the underlying demand remains.

The trade-off: even when reappraising successfully avoids unpleasant emotions, the problem that triggered the appraisal is still there. Emotion-focused coping treats the feeling, not the cause.

What drives the choice
Why people pick one coping strategy over another

1Beliefs

People choose the strategy they believe is most likely to work for the demand they face.

2Priority

Focus energy on the most immediate or important demands; avoid or ignore others for now.

3Control

Belief that a strategy gives control over the stressor favors problem-focused coping.

4Hindrance

People feel less control over hindrances than challenges — pushing them toward emotion-focused coping.

Example — “Trouble with boss.” You'd want to change your boss's behavior (problem-focused), but bosses hold power and complaining upward may not be an option. The resulting anxiety and hopelessness erode control, steering you toward emotion-focused coping.

The Experience of Strain

How exactly does stress cause strain?

The very mechanism that helps your body perform under demand is the one that harms you when demands don't ramp down.

PhysiologicalPsychologicalBehavioralBurnout
Case + Mechanism
When adaptation turns toxic

RIVANaomi Henderson, CEO

Working 120 hours a week, she woke one night literally unable to move — paralyzed. The ER diagnosis: stress. Prescription: bed rest, 14 hours a day, for six weeks.

⚙The body's response

Under a stressor, the body secretes chemicals that raise heart rate and blood pressure, redirecting blood to the brain and muscles. If those chemicals stay elevated from prolonged exposure, the body begins to break down.

The same mechanism that lets you function under pressure is what ultimately causes the exhaustion, irritability, and aches of heavy stress. Those breakdowns come in three varieties →
IS 340 · Chapter 5 — StressThe experience of strain · pp. 132–134
Figure 5-2
Three varieties of strain

♡Physiological

Illness, high blood pressure, coronary artery disease, headaches, back pain, stomachaches.

◈Psychological

Depression, anxiety, irritability, forgetfulness, inability to think clearly, reduced confidence, burnout.

◐Behavioral

Alcohol and drug use, teeth grinding, compulsive behaviors, overeating.

Don't dismiss it because you're young: high-pressure deadlines raise the chance of a heart attack in the next 24 hours sixfold (+600%), and stress measured today can surface as chronic disease eight years later.

Psychological Strain
Burnout: the strain that ends careers
Burnout
The emotional, mental, and physical exhaustion that results from having to cope with stressful demands on an ongoing basis.

🏈Brett Favre's revolving door

After 17 seasons he retired in 2008, saying he was “just tired of all the stress.” He un-retired, re-retired, un-retired again — a familiar pattern after leaving an exciting job to burnout.

↺Why the break tempts a return

A break from work stressors lets a person rest and recharge — but it also gives free time to remember the excitement and challenge of performing, luring them back.

Psychological strains also include reduced self-confidence, lack of creativity, memory loss, and — no surprise given the list — a loss of one's sense of humor.

Learning Goal Four

How does the Type A Behavior Pattern influence the stress process?

People differ in how they react to demands. Three individual factors — Type A, recovery, and social support — reshape the whole stress process.

Type A vs. Type BRecoverySocial support
Individual Differences
The Type A Behavior Pattern
Type A
A strong sense of time urgency — impatient, hard-driving, competitive, controlling, aggressive, and even hostile. If you walk, talk, and eat quickly and get annoyed with slow people, chances are you lean Type A.

Type A

Perceives higher stress levels and is more sensitive to the stress experienced.

Type B

Senses less stress in life and is less sensitive to the stress that is experienced.

You'll take the 12-item Type A questionnaire yourself in two slides. Sum ≥ 60 leans Type A; ≤ 36 leans Type B.
IS 340 · Chapter 5 — StressType A Behavior Pattern · p. 135
Why Type A matters
Type A shapes stressors, stress, and strains

1More stressors

Hard-driving Type A behaviors get rewarded with more work, and their aggressiveness invites interpersonal conflict — a major stressor.

2More stress

Competitiveness makes them hypersensitive to demands, so they appraise more demands as stressful rather than benign.

3More strains

Directly linked to coronary heart disease and other physiological, psychological, and behavioral strains.

The link to heart disease isn't strong enough to say “call 911 if you're Type A” — but it does mean the risk of these problems is significantly higher for people who habitually engage in Type A behavior.

Second Individual Factor
Recovery: not all downtime is equal
Recovery
The degree to which energies used for coping with work demands are replenished by a period of rest or relief from work. Deplete it without restocking and tomorrow's demands feel even more stressful.

↑Restores energy

Relaxing leisure — seeing friends, reading, watching TV, going for a hike. Quality sleep also lowers anxiety, depression, and fatigue.

↓Drains energy

Effortful off-work activity — household chores, grocery shopping, driving kids around, prepping for work. These require physical, cognitive, or emotional effort.

The key insight: “time off” only helps if it's genuinely restorative. A weekend of errands can leave you as depleted as a workday.
IS 340 · Chapter 5 — StressRecovery · p. 137
Third Individual Factor
Social support comes in two forms
Social support
The help people receive when confronted with stressful demands — from coworkers, supervisors, family, and friends.

🛠Instrumental support

Help that addresses the demand directly — a coworker taking over some of your overload or showing you a faster way to do the work.

🤝Emotional support

Help with the emotional distress that accompanies the demand — a supervisor showing interest and being understanding and sympathetic.

Social support is a “moderator.” The stressor–strain relationship is weaker at high support and stronger at low support — the buffering effect you'll test next.

Learning Goal Five

How does stress affect job performance and organizational commitment?

The type of stressor flips the story — hindrance stressors hurt, challenge stressors help.

Job performanceOrganizational commitmentPresenteeism
Figure 5-4
Hindrance stressors: harmful on both outcomes

↓Job performance

Weak negative (≈ −.10). Hindrances drain the physical, cognitive, and emotional energy people bring to their duties — and may trigger counterproductive behavior.

↓↓Organizational commitment

Strong negative (≈ −.50). Hindrances cause dissatisfaction; people who feel constantly sick and exhausted want to leave. Affective & normative commitment fall hardest.

Legend for the tool ahead: a weak correlation ≈ .10, moderate ≈ .30, and strong ≈ .50 in magnitude.
IS 340 · Chapter 5 — StressLePine, Podsakoff & LePine · p. 139
Figure 5-5
Challenge stressors: beneficial on balance

↑Job performance

Weak positive (≈ +.10). Challenges still cause strain, but they trigger positive emotions and problem-focused coping typical of highly engaged employees.

↑↑Organizational commitment

Moderate positive (≈ +.30). The net benefit of engagement outweighs the added strain — raising affective & normative commitment.

The long-term catch — presenteeism: because challenge-loaded employees are satisfied and engaged, they come to work sick, spreading illness and spiraling into impaired health.

Step One
Assessment: run a stress audit
Before acting, managers estimate whether stress is a problem by asking three categories of questions about the jobs in their organization.

1Change

Is the organization going through changes — a merger, or a shift to team-based work — that raise uncertainty about job security or how contributions are rewarded?

2The work itself

What is the level and type of stressors employees face day to day?

3Relationships

What is the quality of relationships among employees and with the organization — and do organizational politics drive administrative decisions?

Only after a stress audit reveals a problem does it make sense to choose among the interventions ahead: managing stressors vs. reducing strains.
IS 340 · Chapter 5 — StressAssessment · pp. 141–142
Approach A
Reducing stressors at the source

✂Limit demands

Xonex Relocation bans working through lunch and structures workflow so people don't leave with unfinished work hanging over them.

⇆Job sharing

Two people share one job as a single unit (used by 19% of orgs). Two Fleet Bank women shared a VP role — one desk, one goal set — for six years.

🏝Sabbaticals

Time off for an alternative activity (≈11% paid, ~⅓ unpaid). American Express and PwC offer up to six months.

The limitation: sabbaticals and vacations give a clean break, but because the stressors in the job never change, the employee likely returns to the same level of stress.

Providing resources & supportive practices

🎓Training interventions

More competencies and skills let employees handle more demands before appraising them as taxing — and build a sense of control that fosters problem-focused coping.

🗓Supportive practices

Help employees balance roles. From a survey of ~400 organizations:

Approach C
Reducing strains directly

🧘Relaxation techniques

Progressive muscle relaxation, meditation, deep breathing. Herbert Benson: repeat a word or phrase for 10–20 minutes, once or twice a day.

💓Biofeedback

A heart monitor and software teach people to shift heart rhythms from an anxious to a calmer pattern. BlueCross BlueShield of Tennessee trained ~⅕ of its 4,500 employees.

🗣Cognitive-behavioral

“Self-talk” replaces doom with rational optimism — “this demand isn't so tough.” Freescale trains 6,000 employees to be resilient.

The three-part playbook: reduce or eliminate stressors, provide resources to cope, or reduce the strains directly. Build your own plan next.

Six takeaways
5.1
Stress is a psychological response when a demand at stake would tax or exceed one's resources. Stressors cause it; strains are its consequences.

Stressors are challenge (growth) or hindrance (hurdles), found in both work and nonwork domains.
5.3
Coping addresses one of two goals: managing the demand or easing the emotional discomfort.

5.6
Organizations reduce stressors, provide resources to cope, or reduce strains — because stress is costly.
